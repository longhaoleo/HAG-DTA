\abstract{\textbf{Motivation}: Accurate prediction of the affinity between drugs and target proteins is important for the drug development process, and deep learning methods have emerged as significant tools in drug-target affinity prediction (DTA). However, the existing DTA models ignore the impact of specific structural features within drug molecules, leading to inadequate representation of the drug molecule characteristics.\newline
\textbf{Results}: We propose the HAG-DTA model to improve the DTA prediction accuracy by sufficiently utilizing multi-scale features of drugs, in which the multi-scale features are extracted by two modules. \section{Introduction}

Drug-target affinity (DTA) prediction plays a critical role in computer-aided drug discovery, as it enables the estimation of binding strength between small-molecule compounds and target proteins. Accurate DTA prediction is essential for a wide range of downstream applications, including virtual screening, drug repositioning, and lead compound optimization. However, experimental determination of binding affinity is often time-consuming, labor-intensive, and costly \cite{ozturk2018deepdta}. In this background, computational approaches for DTA prediction have become increasingly important, as they allow large-scale screening of candidate compounds and help prioritize promising drug-target pairs for further experimental validation.

With the rapid development of deep learning, various neural network-based methods have been proposed to improve DTA prediction. Early deep learning models encode drugs and proteins as one-dimensional sequences, enabling end-to-end representation learning, such as DeepDTA \cite{ozturk2018deepdta}. More recently, graph neural network (GNN)-based approaches have been widely adopted to represent drugs as molecular graphs, such as GraphDTA \cite{nguyen2021graphdta}, allowing the incorporation of structural information into DTA prediction. These methods have demonstrated strong performance improvements by capturing local topological patterns of molecules and sequence-level features of proteins.

Despite these advances, several limitations remain. On the one hand, most existing methods focus on single-scale molecular representations or implicitly capture multi-scale information without explicitly modeling the hierarchical structure of molecules. In practice, drug molecules naturally exhibit hierarchical organization, where atoms form functional groups and functional groups further compose molecular fragments. Although some studies have explored multi-scale graph learning \cite{yang2022mgraphdta,ying2018hierarchical}, the explicit modeling of hierarchical molecular relationships remains insufficient, which may limit the ability of models to capture chemically meaningful patterns associated with drug-target binding.

On the other hand, interpretability remains a critical yet underexplored aspect in many DTA prediction frameworks. In practical drug discovery, the goal is not only to predict binding affinity accurately, but also to identify key atoms, functional groups, or molecular fragments that contribute to binding. However, existing deep learning-based methods often act as black-box models, and their decision-making processes are difficult to interpret. Although attention-based mechanisms have been introduced to provide partial explanations \cite{zhao2023attentiondta}, they typically offer coarse-grained insights and do not explicitly reveal how molecular information is organized across different structural levels.

To address these challenges, we propose a Hierarchical Attention-based Graph model for Drug-Target Affinity prediction (HAG-DTA). The proposed model explicitly learns hierarchical molecular representations by organizing drug features into atom-level, functional group-level, and fragment-level structures. In addition, a deep graph representation branch is introduced to capture global molecular topology, while a convolutional neural network is employed to extract protein sequence features. These heterogeneous features are then integrated through an attention-based fusion mechanism, which dynamically assigns importance to different feature components. By combining hierarchical structural modeling with adaptive feature fusion, the proposed model not only improves prediction performance but also enhances structural interpretability.

Extensive experiments on multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate that HAG-DTA achieves competitive performance in both regression and classification tasks. Furthermore, ablation studies, hyperparameter analyses, and visualization results provide additional insights into the effectiveness and interpretability of the proposed model.


\section{Related Work}\label{sec2}

\subsection{Drug-Target Affinity Prediction Methods}

Drug-target affinity (DTA) prediction is a fundamental task in computer-aided drug design. It aims to estimate the binding strength between small-molecule compounds and target proteins, thereby supporting virtual screening, drug repositioning, and candidate compound optimization. Early DTA prediction studies mainly relied on handcrafted molecular descriptors, protein sequence similarities, and traditional machine learning models. Representative methods include KronRLS \cite{pahikkala2015toward}, which formulates drug-target interaction prediction with Kronecker regularized least squares, and SimBoost \cite{He2017SimBoost}, which predicts continuous binding affinity values using gradient boosting with drug-target similarity features. However, the performance of these methods largely depends on feature engineering, and their ability to capture complex drug-target interactions is limited.

With the rapid development of deep learning, neural networks have been increasingly used to automatically learn expressive representations of drugs and proteins. DeepDTA \cite{ozturk2018deepdta} first applied convolutional neural networks to DTA prediction by encoding SMILES-based drug strings and protein sequences in an end-to-end manner. WideDTA \cite{ozturk2019widedta} further introduced multiple textual representations of drugs and proteins, while AttentionDTA \cite{zhao2023attentiondta} incorporated attention mechanisms to enhance the modeling of important sequence regions. In addition, TransformerCPI \cite{chen2020transformercpi} and MolTrans \cite{huang2021moltrans} explored self-attention and Transformer-based architectures to capture compound-protein interaction patterns. These methods demonstrate the effectiveness of deep learning in DTA prediction and reduce the dependence on manually designed features.

Nevertheless, sequence-based methods usually treat SMILES strings as one-dimensional sequences. As a result, they cannot fully exploit the intrinsic topological structures of molecules. To address this limitation, graph neural network (GNN)-based models have been widely adopted in recent years. GraphDTA \cite{nguyen2021graphdta} represents drugs as molecular graphs and uses graph convolutional networks to learn drug features, achieving improved prediction performance. Based on this idea, MGraphDTA and DGraphDTA \cite{yang2022mgraphdta,jiang2022sequence} further incorporate multi-scale topological information and spatial structural features to improve molecular representation. AttentionMGT-DTA \cite{wu2024attentionmgtdta} further combines graph Transformer and attention mechanisms for multimodal DTA prediction. These studies indicate that molecular graph modeling is essential for accurate DTA prediction.

Recently, more advanced DTA models have been proposed to further improve molecular and protein representation learning. GPCNDTA \cite{zhang2023gpcndta} introduces pharmacophore prior information and modal interaction strategies to enhance drug-target feature extraction. GEFormerDTA \cite{liu2024geformerdta} introduces a Transformer graph-based early fusion strategy and reports competitive results on the Davis and KIBA benchmark datasets. GNPDTA \cite{ye2024graphpre} employs graph neural pre-training to extract low-level features from drug atom graphs and target residue graphs. GLCN-DTA \cite{qi2024extendedgraph} enhances DTA prediction by combining graph learning operations with graph convolution operations. CSCo-DTA \cite{wang2024crossscale} integrates molecule-scale and network-scale information through cross-scale graph contrastive learning. MDCT-DTA \cite{zhu2024multiscale} introduces multi-scale diffusion and interactive learning for DTA prediction. WPGraphDTA \cite{hu2025powergraph} constructs drug-target affinity representations based on power graph and Word2Vec. MEGDTA \cite{hou2025megdta} further incorporates protein three-dimensional structural information and ensemble graph neural networks to capture diverse drug and target features. These recent studies indicate that richer structural information, pre-training strategies, contrastive learning, and more effective fusion mechanisms have become important directions in DTA prediction. However, how to explicitly organize molecular representations across atom-level, functional group-level, and fragment-level structures remains insufficiently explored.


\subsection{Graph Neural Network-Based Molecular Representation Learning}

Graph neural networks have become powerful tools for molecular representation learning. They can directly model atoms as nodes and chemical bonds as edges. In this way, both atom attributes and molecular topology can be captured.
\begin{itemize}
\item Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) \cite{kipf2016semi} update node representations through normalized neighborhood aggregation. Each node aggregates information from itself and its neighboring nodes, which enables the model to capture local structural patterns. GCNs have a simple architecture and stable training behavior. However, when many layers are stacked, node representations may become overly similar, leading to the over-smoothing problem.

\item Graph Isomorphism Networks (GINs) \cite{xu2018powerful} improve the discriminative power of GNNs by using sum-based aggregation and multilayer perceptrons. This design allows GINs to preserve neighborhood multiset information and provides strong expressive capability comparable to the Weisfeiler-Lehman graph isomorphism test. Therefore, GINs are suitable for molecular tasks that require fine-grained structural distinction. However, they may also be sensitive to noise and prone to overfitting.

\item Graph Attention Networks (GATs) \cite{velivckovic2017graph} introduce an attention mechanism into neighborhood aggregation. By assigning different attention weights to neighboring nodes, GATs can adaptively focus on more informative local structures. This improves modeling flexibility and provides a certain degree of interpretability. The main limitation is that GATs usually require higher computational costs.

\item GraphSAGE \cite{hamilton2017inductive} learns inductive node representations through neighborhood sampling and aggregation. It does not require access to the entire graph during training. This property enables GraphSAGE to generalize to unseen nodes or graphs, making it useful for large-scale and dynamic graph scenarios. Its performance is closely related to the sampling strategy and aggregation function.
\end{itemize}

To further enhance molecular representation learning, several studies have explored multi-scale and hierarchical graph modeling strategies \cite{ying2018hierarchical,yang2022mgraphdta,wang2024crossscale,qi2024extendedgraph}. These methods aim to capture both local substructures and global topological information. DiffPool \cite{ying2018hierarchical} provides a differentiable graph pooling framework that learns node-to-cluster assignments and generates hierarchical graph representations. MGraphDTA \cite{yang2022mgraphdta} employs deep multiscale graph neural networks to learn molecular representations from different receptive fields. CSCo-DTA \cite{wang2024crossscale} integrates molecule-scale and network-scale information through cross-scale graph contrastive learning, while GLCN-DTA \cite{qi2024extendedgraph} improves DTA prediction by extending graph learning operations. These studies suggest that multi-scale graph modeling is important for capturing complex molecular structures.

Despite these advances, most existing DTA models still focus on single-scale graph representations or shallow hierarchical structures. Although multi-scale graph learning can expand the receptive field of molecular representation, the fine-grained relationships among atoms, functional groups, and molecular fragments are not sufficiently modeled. In particular, many methods lack an explicit mechanism to learn how atom-level information is progressively organized into higher-level molecular substructures. This limitation may reduce the ability of existing models to represent chemically meaningful structural patterns related to drug-target binding.


\subsection{Multimodal Fusion and Model Interpretability Analysis}

In DTA prediction, drugs and proteins belong to heterogeneous modalities. Effective feature fusion is therefore essential for modeling drug-target interactions. Existing studies commonly fuse drug and protein representations through feature concatenation or attention mechanisms. For example, AttentionDTA \cite{zhao2023attentiondta} uses attention weights to highlight important features and improve prediction accuracy. TransformerCPI \cite{chen2020transformercpi} applies self-attention mechanisms to learn compound-protein interaction features, while MolTrans \cite{huang2021moltrans} introduces a knowledge-inspired substructure mining strategy and interaction modeling module. AttentionMGT-DTA \cite{wu2024attentionmgtdta} further adopts graph Transformer and attention mechanisms to integrate multimodal drug and target information. These studies show that attention-based fusion can improve the interaction modeling ability of DTA models. However, simple concatenation may not sufficiently capture the relative contribution of different drug and protein features, while attention-based fusion still requires more explicit structural guidance.

In addition, multimodal learning has also been applied to other pharmaceutical prediction tasks. DPSP \cite{masumshah2023dpsp} and NNPS \cite{masumshah2021polypharmacyside}, for example, propose deep learning frameworks for polypharmacy side-effect prediction, showing the potential of integrating heterogeneous drug-related information. These studies indicate that the combination of chemical structures, biological targets, side effects, and pathway information can improve the representation ability of deep learning models in drug-related prediction tasks.

Model interpretability has also attracted increasing attention. In DTA prediction, interpretability is important because it can help identify key atoms, functional groups, or protein regions that contribute to binding affinity. MONN \cite{li2020monn} predicts both compound-protein interactions and binding affinities, and explores attention-based interpretation of molecular interactions. TransformerCPI \cite{chen2020transformercpi} maps attention weights to protein sequences and compound atoms to support interaction interpretation. MolTrans \cite{huang2021moltrans} further provides interpretable substructure-level interaction modeling. Attention weight analysis and molecular substructure visualization are commonly used for this purpose. These techniques can provide useful clues for rational drug design.

Although existing methods have improved feature fusion and interpretability, several limitations remain. First, multi-scale molecular structural information is still insufficiently utilized. Second, the correlations among different structural levels are rarely modeled explicitly. Third, the interpretation of model decision-making is often coarse and lacks detailed structural evidence. These limitations motivate the development of models that can jointly learn hierarchical molecular representations, integrate drug and protein features adaptively, and provide more informative structural visualization.

To address these challenges, we propose a \textbf{Hierarchical Attention-based Graph model for Drug-Target Affinity prediction (HAG-DTA)}. In addition to improving prediction accuracy, HAG-DTA is designed to enhance structural interpretability by explicitly modeling hierarchical molecular representations. Specifically, the model progressively learns atom-level, functional group-level, and molecular fragment-level features, allowing the contribution of different molecular substructures to be visualized and analyzed. These hierarchical drug representations are further integrated with global molecular topology and protein sequence features through an attention-based fusion mechanism.


\section{Materials and methods}\label{sec3}

\subsection{Problem formulation}\label{subsec:problem_formulation}

Drug-target affinity (DTA) prediction aims to estimate the interaction strength between a drug molecule and a protein target from their structural and sequential information. Given a drug molecular graph $G_d$ and a protein sequence $S_p$, the objective of the model is to learn a mapping function:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}=f(G_d,S_p),
\label{eq:problem_formulation}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{y}$ denotes the predicted output. For the Davis and KIBA datasets, DTA prediction is formulated as a regression task, where $y\in\mathbb{R}$ represents a continuous binding affinity value. For the Human and \textit{C.elegans} datasets, the prediction task is formulated as a binary classification task, where $y\in\{0,1\}$ indicates whether an interaction exists between a drug and a target protein.

\subsection{Input representation}\label{subsec:input_representation}

\subsubsection{Drug molecular graph representation}\label{subsubsec:drug_graph_representation}

Drug molecules are originally represented by SMILES (Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System) strings. Following the molecular graph construction strategy used in GraphDTA\cite{nguyen2021graphdta}, we convert each SMILES string into an undirected molecular graph using RDKit\cite{landrum2006rdkit}. A drug molecule is represented as $G_d=(V,E,X)$, where $V$ denotes the set of atom nodes, $E$ denotes the set of chemical bonds, and $X\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$ denotes the node feature matrix. Here, $n$ is the number of atoms and $d$ is the dimensionality of the atom feature vector. The conversion from a SMILES string to a molecular graph is illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig2}(b).

For each atom node, a 78-dimensional feature vector is constructed to describe its basic chemical properties. The atom features include atom type, atom degree, number of hydrogen atoms, implicit valence, and aromaticity. The atom type is encoded by one-hot encoding and contains common elements such as C, N, O, S, F, P, Cl, and Br, together with an Unknown category. The atom degree, the number of hydrogen atoms, and the implicit valence are also encoded by one-hot encoding according to values from 0 to 10. Aromaticity is represented by a Boolean indicator. To reduce the influence of feature-scale differences on model training, the atom feature vector is normalized after construction.

For edge construction, chemical bonds in the molecule are used to define undirected edges. Each chemical bond is transformed into two directed edges to support message passing in graph neural networks. Self-loops are further added to preserve the information of the central atom during neighborhood aggregation. Since different drug molecules contain different numbers of atoms, the molecular graphs are transformed into dense graph representations with fixed node sizes. For the Davis dataset and the KIBA molecules with no more than 46 atoms, the molecular graph is padded to 46 nodes. For the KIBA molecules with more than 46 atoms, the maximum node number is set to 268 to preserve the structural information of large molecules. The resulting dense graph representation is used as the input of the hierarchical graph pooling module and the deep graph neural network module.

\subsubsection{Protein sequence representation}\label{subsubsec:protein_sequence_representation}

A protein sequence consists of amino acid residues. We construct an amino acid dictionary using the character set ``ABCDEFGHIKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ'' and map each residue character to a corresponding integer index. To ensure consistent input dimensions during batch training, all protein sequences are truncated or padded to a fixed length of 1000.

The encoded protein sequence is first fed into an embedding layer and mapped into a continuous vector representation. Then, a convolutional neural network is used to extract local sequence patterns from the embedded protein representation. The extracted protein feature is subsequently fused with drug molecular features for DTA prediction.

\subsection{Overall architecture of HAG-DTA}\label{subsec:overall_architecture}

The proposed HAG-DTA model is an end-to-end DTA prediction framework. As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig1}, the model consists of four feature extraction branches and an attention-based prediction module. The four feature branches include the functional group-level feature extracted by the first hierarchical graph pooling layer, the molecular fragment-level feature extracted by the second hierarchical graph pooling layer, the global molecular topological feature extracted by the deep graph neural network, and the protein sequence feature extracted by the convolutional neural network.

In this study, the term multi-scale molecular feature refers to the representations of a drug molecule at different structural levels, including the atom-level input representation, the functional group-level representation, the molecular fragment-level representation, and the global topological representation. The term hierarchical graph pooling refers to a learnable graph coarsening process based on assignment matrices. Through this process, the model learns the mapping relationship from low-level nodes to high-level structural nodes and progressively abstracts the atom-level molecular graph into higher-level structural representations.

HAG-DTA first extracts hierarchical structural features and global graph features from the original molecular graph in parallel. At the same time, it extracts local sequential patterns from the protein sequence. Then, four 128-dimensional feature vectors are fed into the attention-based fusion module. The attention block adaptively learns the contribution weight of each feature branch according to the prediction task. The fused representation is finally passed through two fully connected layers and an output layer to obtain the predicted affinity value or interaction probability.

\subsection{Hierarchical graph representation learning module(HGNN)}\label{subsec:hierarchical_graph_module}

Drug molecules naturally have hierarchical structures. Atoms are connected by chemical bonds to form local chemical structures, and these local structures further constitute higher-level molecular fragments. To capture such progressive structural information, we design a two-layer hierarchical graph pooling module to coarsen the drug molecular graph. This process is illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig2}(c).

We adopt DiffPool\cite{ying2018hierarchical} to implement learnable hierarchical graph pooling. For the $l$-th pooling layer, the input consists of an adjacency matrix $A^{(l)}$ and a node feature matrix $X^{(l)}$. The layer contains an embedding network and a pooling network. The embedding network generates the node embedding matrix:
\begin{equation}
Z^{(l)}=GNN_{l,embed}\left(A^{(l)},X^{(l)}\right),
\label{eq:diffpool_embed}
\end{equation}
where $Z^{(l)}\in\mathbb{R}^{n_l\times d_l}$, $n_l$ is the number of input nodes at the $l$-th layer, and $d_l$ is the output feature dimension.

The pooling network generates the assignment matrix from low-level nodes to high-level structural nodes:
\begin{equation}
S^{(l)}=\operatorname{softmax}\left(GNN_{l,pool}\left(A^{(l)},X^{(l)}\right)\right),
\label{eq:diffpool_assignment}
\end{equation}
where $S^{(l)}\in\mathbb{R}^{n_l\times n_{l+1}}$, and $n_{l+1}$ denotes the predefined number of nodes in the coarsened graph. Each row of $S^{(l)}$ represents the assignment probability of a node in the $l$-th graph to the structural nodes in the next-level graph.

The node feature matrix and adjacency matrix of the coarsened graph are then updated according to:
\begin{equation}
X^{(l+1)}={S^{(l)}}^T Z^{(l)},
\label{eq:diffpool_x}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
A^{(l+1)}={S^{(l)}}^T A^{(l)}S^{(l)},
\label{eq:diffpool_a}
\end{equation}
where $X^{(l+1)}$ and $A^{(l+1)}$ represent the feature matrix and adjacency matrix of the coarsened graph, respectively.

The first graph pooling layer compresses the atom-level molecular graph into a higher-level graph with $n_1$ nodes, which is used to approximate functional group-level structural information. The second graph pooling layer further compresses the graph into a higher-level graph with $n_2$ nodes, which is used to characterize molecular fragment-level representation. These two hyperparameters jointly determine the granularity of molecular structural abstraction.

The node features obtained from the first and second pooling layers are processed by flattening and fully connected layers, respectively, and both are mapped into 128-dimensional feature vectors for subsequent multi-scale feature fusion. In addition, DiffPool introduces a link prediction loss and an entropy regularization loss during training. The link prediction loss helps preserve the topological consistency before and after pooling, while the entropy regularization loss encourages clearer node-to-cluster assignments.

\subsection{Deep graph neural network module(DGNN)}\label{subsec:deep_graph_module}

The hierarchical graph pooling module progressively extracts functional group-level and molecular fragment-level representations from the atom-level molecular graph. To further preserve the global topological information of the original molecular graph, we introduce a deep graph neural network module to directly extract global molecular topological features from the original graph. This process is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig2}(a).

The deep graph neural network stacks multiple GNN layers to perform message passing and aggregate information from distant atoms in the molecular graph. In this study, we evaluate four representative GNN variants, including GCN\cite{kipf2016semi}, GraphSAGE\cite{hamilton2017inductive}, GAT\cite{velivckovic2017graph}, and GIN\cite{xu2018powerful}. Taking the GIN version as an example, the global graph branch contains five DenseGINConv layers, and each layer is followed by a ReLU activation function and a GraphNorm layer. For the GCN, GAT, and GraphSAGE versions, DenseGCNConv, DenseGATConv, and DenseSAGEConv are used to construct the same global graph modeling pipeline, respectively.

As the number of graph neural network layers increases, deep GNNs may suffer from information decay, over-smoothing, and vanishing gradients\cite{li2019deepgcns}. To alleviate these problems, we introduce a gated skip-connection mechanism between adjacent layers. This mechanism enables the model to adaptively combine the current-layer graph convolutional feature with the retained feature from the previous layer. The gated update is defined as:
\begin{equation}
Z^{(l)}=\sigma\left(W_1H^{(l)}+W_2\tilde{H}^{(l-1)}+b\right),
\label{eq:gated_z}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\tilde{H}^{(l)}=Z^{(l)}\odot H^{(l)}+\left(1-Z^{(l)}\right)\odot \tilde{H}^{(l-1)},
\label{eq:gated_h}
\end{equation}
where $H^{(l)}$ denotes the node feature output of the $l$-th GNN layer, $\tilde{H}^{(l-1)}$ denotes the retained feature after gated fusion from the previous layer, $Z^{(l)}$ denotes the gate coefficient, $\sigma(\cdot)$ denotes the sigmoid function, $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication, and $W_1$, $W_2$, and $b$ are trainable parameters.


\subsection{Interaction between HGNN and DGNN}
\label{subsec:hgnn_dgnn_interaction}

The HGNN and DGNN modules are designed as two complementary graph representation learning branches. Given the same atom-level molecular graph, the HGNN branch explicitly changes the graph resolution through learnable graph pooling, while the DGNN branch preserves the original molecular topology and increases the receptive field through deep message passing. Therefore, the transition from local structural learning to global topological learning is realized by jointly modeling the molecule from different graph resolutions.

Specifically, the HGNN branch follows a progressive coarsening path:
\begin{equation}
G^{(0)} \rightarrow G^{(1)} \rightarrow G^{(2)},
\end{equation}
where $G^{(0)}$ denotes the original atom-level molecular graph, $G^{(1)}$ denotes the first-level coarsened graph with $n_1$ structural nodes, and $G^{(2)}$ denotes the second-level coarsened graph with $n_2$ structural nodes. In this way, the model first aggregates atom-level information into functional group-level representations and then further abstracts them into molecular fragment-level representations. The two hierarchical graph features are defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}_{H1}=f_{H1}(G^{(1)}), \quad
\mathbf{X}_{H2}=f_{H2}(G^{(2)}),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{h}_{H1}$ and $\mathbf{h}_{H2}$ represent the local hierarchical molecular features extracted from two structural scales.

In parallel, the DGNN branch operates on the original molecular graph $G^{(0)}$ and performs multi-layer message passing without changing the graph resolution. This branch is used to capture long-range atom dependencies and preserve the global molecular topology. The global graph representation extracted by the DGNN branch is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}_{G}=f_{G}(G^{(0)}).
\end{equation}

The three graph-level representations describe the same molecule from different structural perspectives. The hierarchical features $\mathbf{h}_{H1}$ and $\mathbf{h}_{H2}$ emphasize local and mesoscopic chemical substructures, whereas the global feature $\mathbf{h}_{G}$ characterizes the overall topological dependency of the molecular graph. To make these features comparable and suitable for subsequent fusion, they are projected into a shared latent space:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_{H1}=W_{H1}\mathbf{X}_{H1}+b_{H1}, \quad
\mathbf{z}_{H2}=W_{H2}\mathbf{X}_{H2}+b_{H2}, \quad
\mathbf{z}_{G}=W_{G}\mathbf{X}_{G}+b_{G},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{z}_{H1}$, $\mathbf{z}_{H2}$, and $\mathbf{z}_{G}$ are 128-dimensional graph representations.

After this projection, the hierarchical and global graph features are jointly fed into the attention-based fusion module together with the protein sequence representation. The attention mechanism adaptively assigns different importance weights to functional group-level, molecular fragment-level, global molecular topology, and protein sequence features. Therefore, HAG-DTA can integrate local chemical substructures and global molecular dependencies in a unified prediction framework.

In addition, the MMD loss is introduced to reduce the distribution discrepancy between the hierarchical graph features and the global graph feature. This constraint encourages the local structural representations and the global topological representation to be aligned in the shared latent space, thereby improving the consistency of multi-scale molecular representation learning.


\subsection{Protein sequence feature extraction module}\label{subsec:protein_cnn_module}

The protein sequence feature extraction module is used to capture local patterns in amino acid sequences, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig2}(d). The integer-encoded protein sequence with a fixed length of 1000 is first fed into an embedding layer to obtain a sequence embedding matrix with a size of $1000\times128$. The embedded sequence is then expanded into a two-dimensional convolutional input and passed through three convolutional layers with kernel sizes of 3, 4, and 5, respectively.

Each convolutional layer contains 128 output channels and uses the ReLU activation function to generate local sequence features. Then, one-dimensional max pooling is applied to each convolutional output to compress local responses at different sequence positions into a fixed-length vector. The three groups of convolutional features are concatenated into a 384-dimensional vector and then mapped into a 128-dimensional protein representation through a fully connected layer.

This design enables the model to capture amino acid local patterns at different length scales and provides sequence-level information for subsequent drug-protein feature fusion.

\subsection{Attention-based multimodal fusion module}\label{subsec:attention_fusion_module}

After obtaining four 128-dimensional feature vectors, we use an attention mechanism to perform multimodal feature fusion. The four feature vectors are the first-level hierarchical graph feature $x_{H_1}$, the second-level hierarchical graph feature $x_{H_2}$, the protein sequence feature $x_P$, and the global molecular topological feature $x_G$.

For each feature branch, an independent projection network is used to calculate its importance score. Each projection network consists of a linear layer, a Tanh activation function, and a linear scoring layer:
\begin{equation}
e_j=w_j^T\tanh\left(W_jx_j+b_j\right),\quad j\in\{H_1,H_2,P,G\},
\label{eq:attention_score}
\end{equation}
where $x_j$ denotes the feature vector of the $j$-th branch, $e_j$ denotes the corresponding attention score, and $W_j$, $w_j$, and $b_j$ are learnable parameters.

The four scores are concatenated and normalized by the softmax function to obtain attention weights:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_j=\frac{\exp(e_j)}{\sum_{k\in\{H_1,H_2,P,G\}}\exp(e_k)},\quad j\in\{H_1,H_2,P,G\}.
\label{eq:attention_weight}
\end{equation}

The four features are then weighted by their corresponding attention weights and concatenated:
\begin{equation}
x_F=\alpha_{H_1}x_{H_1}\Vert\alpha_{H_2}x_{H_2}\Vert\alpha_Px_P\Vert\alpha_Gx_G,
\label{eq:fusion_feature}
\end{equation}
where $\Vert$ denotes the concatenation operation. The weighted features are reorganized as a $4\times128$ fused representation and then fed into the fully connected prediction layers. This fusion strategy enables the model to dynamically adjust the contribution of each feature branch for different drug-target pairs, thereby improving its ability to represent complex drug-target relationships.

\subsection{Prediction layer and loss function}\label{subsec:prediction_loss}

The fused feature first passes through two fully connected layers. The first fully connected layer maps the $4\times128$ fused feature into a 1024-dimensional representation, followed by BatchNorm, ReLU, and Dropout. The second fully connected layer maps the 1024-dimensional feature into a 256-dimensional representation, followed by the same BatchNorm, ReLU, and Dropout operations. Finally, the output layer maps the 256-dimensional feature into the final prediction. The dropout rate is set to 0.2 in this study.

For the Davis and KIBA regression tasks, the basic prediction loss is the mean squared error (MSE):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{MSE}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(y_i-\hat{y}_i\right)^2,
\label{eq:mse_loss}
\end{equation}
where $N$ denotes the number of samples, $y_i$ denotes the ground-truth affinity value of the $i$-th sample, and $\hat{y}_i$ denotes the predicted affinity value.

The total training objective also contains the link prediction loss and entropy regularization loss generated by DiffPool, as well as the MMD loss used to reduce the distribution discrepancy between the local hierarchical representation and the global graph representation. The total loss for the regression task is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{MSE}+\alpha\sum_l\left(\mathcal{L}_{link}^{(l)}+\mathcal{L}_{entropy}^{(l)}\right)+\beta\mathcal{L}_{MMD},
\label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{link}^{(l)}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{entropy}^{(l)}$ denote the structure-preserving loss and assignment entropy loss produced by the $l$-th graph pooling layer, respectively. $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are the weights of the auxiliary loss terms. In this study, both $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are set to 0.05.

The link prediction loss is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{link}^{(l)}=\left\|A^{(l)}-S^{(l)}{S^{(l)}}^T\right\|_F,
\label{eq:link_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_F$ denotes the Frobenius norm. This loss encourages the assignment matrix to preserve the topological relationship of the graph before pooling.

The entropy regularization loss is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{entropy}^{(l)}=\frac{1}{n_l}\sum_{i=1}^{n_l}H\left(S_i^{(l)}\right),
\label{eq:entropy_loss}
\end{equation}
where $H(\cdot)$ denotes the entropy function, and $S_i^{(l)}$ denotes the $i$-th row of the assignment matrix $S^{(l)}$. This term encourages each node to have a clearer cluster assignment.

The MMD loss measures the distribution discrepancy between the second-level hierarchical graph feature $x_{H_2}$ and the global graph feature $x_G$. It is computed using a multi-kernel Gaussian kernel, with the number of kernels set to 5 and the kernel bandwidth multiplier set to 2.0. Its empirical form is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\mathcal{L}_{MMD} = &\frac{1}{N(N-1)}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j\neq i}^{N} k\left(x_G^{(i)},x_G^{(j)}\right)
+\frac{1}{N(N-1)}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j\neq i}^{N} k\left(x_{H_2}^{(i)},x_{H_2}^{(j)}\right)\\
&-\frac{2}{N^2}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}k\left(x_G^{(i)},x_{H_2}^{(j)}\right),
\end{split}
\label{eq:mmd_loss}
\end{equation}
where $x_G^{(i)}$ and $x_{H_2}^{(i)}$ denote the global graph feature and the second-level hierarchical graph feature of the $i$-th drug molecule, respectively, and $k(\cdot,\cdot)$ denotes the Gaussian kernel function. This constraint reduces the distribution discrepancy between the hierarchical structural feature and the global topological feature, thereby stabilizing the multi-branch fusion process.

For the Human and \textit{C.elegans} classification tasks, the model output is converted into an interaction probability by the sigmoid function, and binary cross-entropy is used for training:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{BCE}=-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left[y_i\log\left(\hat{p}_i\right)+\left(1-y_i\right)\log\left(1-\hat{p}_i\right)\right],
\label{eq:bce_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}_i$ denotes the predicted probability that the $i$-th drug-target pair has an interaction, and $y_i$ denotes the ground-truth label.

\subsection{Experience details}

\subsubsection{Datasets and data splits}

To comprehensively evaluate the performance of HAG-DTA in drug-target affinity prediction, we conducted experiments on four public datasets, including two regression datasets, Davis and KIBA, and two binary classification datasets, Human and \textit{C. elegans}. Davis and KIBA were used to evaluate the ability of the model to predict continuous drug-target binding affinity values, whereas Human and \textit{C. elegans} were used to evaluate the ability of the model to identify drug-target interaction relationships.

The Davis dataset contains 442 kinase proteins and 68 corresponding inhibitors. 
The binding affinity is represented by the dissociation constant $K_d$, whose values range from 0.016 to 10000. 
Since the original affinity values span a wide numerical range, we followed previous studies and transformed $K_d$ into the logarithmic scale according to Eq\eqref{pkd}.
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{p}K_d=-\log_{10}\left(\frac{K_d}{10^9}\right).
\label{pkd}
\end{equation}
The KIBA dataset contains 2111 drugs and 229 targets. 
Its labels are represented by KIBA scores, with values ranging from 0.0 to 17.2. 
Compared with Davis, KIBA contains a larger number of drug-target interaction samples, which further evaluates the predictive performance of the model on a larger-scale benchmark.

The Human and \textit{C. elegans} datasets consist of positive samples from DrugBank and Matador, together with high-confidence negative samples. They were used for binary drug-target interaction prediction. In our experiments, the original txt files were retained, and a unified training, validation, and test split was adopted to ensure that HAG-DTA and TransformerCPI were evaluated on a consistent data basis.

\subsubsection{Evaluation metrics}

For regression tasks, we used mean squared error (MSE), concordance index (CI), and $r_m^2$ as evaluation metrics. MSE measures the average error between the predicted and true affinity values, where a smaller value indicates lower prediction error. CI measures the ranking consistency of the model among different drug-target pairs. Its value ranges from 0 to 1, and a larger value indicates better ranking consistency. The $r_m^2$ metric is used to evaluate the external predictive performance of the model.

Eq.~\eqref{ci} presents the calculation of CI:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CI}=\frac{1}{Z}\sum_{\delta_i>\delta_j} f(b_i-b_j),
\label{ci}
\end{equation}
where the affinity value of $\delta_i$ is larger than that of $\delta_j$. Here, $b_i$ and $b_j$ denote the predicted values for $\delta_i$ and $\delta_j$, respectively, and $Z$ denotes the normalization constant. The function $f(\cdot)$ is defined as follows:
\begin{equation}
f(x)=
\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if } x>0,\\
0.5, & \text{if } x=0,\\
0, & \text{if } x<0.
\end{cases}
\label{equation_ci_function}
\end{equation}

The $r_m^2$ metric, proposed in DeepDTA~\cite{ozturk2018deepdta}, is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
r_m^2=r^2 \times \left(1-\sqrt{r^2-r_0^2}\right),
\label{rm2}
\end{equation}
where $r$ and $r_0$ are the correlation coefficients with and without intercepts, respectively.

For binary classification tasks, we used AUROC, AUPRC, Precision, and Recall as evaluation metrics. AUROC measures the overall discriminative ability of the model. AUPRC is more suitable for evaluating model performance under imbalanced class distributions. Precision reflects the accuracy of predicted positive samples, while Recall reflects the coverage of true positive samples.


\subsubsection{Implementation details and hyperparameter settings}

All main experiments were repeated using five random seeds, namely 100, 1000, 2000, 3000, and 4000. The final results are reported as mean $\pm$ standard deviation over five runs. For regression tasks, the best epoch was selected according to MSE, while for classification tasks, the best epoch was selected according to AUROC.

All experiments were implemented using PyTorch 2.0.0, PyTorch Geometric 2.6.1, and CUDA 11.8. The model was trained using the Adam optimizer. The learning rate was set to $5\times10^{-4}$, the batch size for both training and evaluation was set to 512, and the maximum number of training epochs was set to 1000. To ensure a fair comparison, the common training hyperparameters were kept consistent with GraphDTA~\cite{nguyen2021graphdta} as much as possible, thereby reducing the influence of training strategy differences on model comparison.

For drug molecular graph encoding, GINConv, GCNConv, GATConv, and SAGEConv were adopted as graph convolution operators to evaluate the influence of different GNN backbones on HAG-DTA. Protein sequences were truncated or padded to a fixed length of 1000 and then fed into the TextCNN module for feature extraction. In the hierarchical GNN module, two DiffPool layers were used to generate two coarsened graphs. The first coarsened graph is designed to capture functional group-level representations, while the second coarsened graph is used to capture higher-level molecular fragment representations.

Since the numbers of nodes in the two coarsened graphs need to be predefined, they are important structural hyperparameters in HAG-DTA. In the main experiments, the settings of $n_1$ and $n_2$ were specified for each dataset. For the regression task, $n_1=4$ and $n_2=2$ were used on the Davis dataset, while $n_1=6$ and $n_2=2$ were used on the KIBA dataset. For the binary classification task, $n_1=7$ and $n_2=3$ were used on both the Human and \textit{C. elegans} datasets. The effects of $n_1$ and $n_2$ were further discussed in the hyperparameter sensitivity analysis. The default weight of the MMD loss was set to $\beta=0.05$, and its influence was analyzed in the ablation study. Table~\ref{table2} summarizes the main hyperparameters used in this study. The final three rows list the candidate structural settings and GNN backbones considered in the model analysis.

\subsection{Comparisons with baseline methods}

Although the preceding section provides an overall comparison with a broad range of existing methods, the most direct baseline for HAG-DTA is GraphDTA\cite{nguyen2021graphdta}. Both methods follow the same basic DTA modeling framework, in which the drug molecule is represented as a graph and the target protein is represented as a sequence. Therefore, the comparison with GraphDTA is particularly important for evaluating whether the proposed hierarchical graph representation and multi-scale fusion strategy improve the original graph-based DTA framework.

GraphDTA uses a single graph neural network to extract drug molecular graph features and a one-dimensional convolutional neural network to extract protein sequence features. The two representations are then concatenated and passed through fully connected layers for affinity prediction. This design allows GraphDTA to preserve molecular topology more effectively than methods based only on molecular fingerprints or SMILES strings, making it a strong and appropriate graph-based baseline for HAG-DTA.

Compared with GraphDTA, HAG-DTA extends the drug-side encoder in three main aspects. First, the hierarchical graph neural network module progressively learns atom-level, functional group-level, and molecular fragment-level representations through graph coarsening, thereby capturing local chemical substructures at multiple scales. Second, the deep graph neural network module extracts global molecular topology and complements the local hierarchical representation. Third, the attention-based fusion module adaptively integrates hierarchical graph features, deep graph features, and protein sequence features, allowing the model to assign different importance to different information sources for different drug-target pairs.

TransformerCPI\cite{chen2020transformercpi} is considered as a supplementary baseline rather than a structurally homologous baseline. It represents sequence-attention-based CPI prediction methods and mainly relies on self-attention to model latent associations between compound and protein sequences. Including TransformerCPI helps evaluate whether explicit molecular graph modeling can provide additional advantages over sequence-centered interaction modeling. Unlike TransformerCPI, HAG-DTA explicitly preserves atomic connectivity and molecular topology, and further extracts functional group-level and fragment-level structural features through hierarchical graph learning.

For the regression task, HAG-DTA(GIN) was compared with GraphDTA(GIN) on Davis. For the classification task, HAG-DTA(GIN) was compared with TransformerCPI on Human and \textit{C. elegans}. \subsection{Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis}

The key hyperparameters in HAG-DTA mainly include the structural hyperparameters $n_1$ and $n_2$ in the hierarchical graph pooling module, and the weight coefficient $\beta$ of the MMD loss. Specifically, $n_1$ and $n_2$ denote the numbers of nodes retained in the two coarsened graphs, respectively, which directly determine the representation granularity when the drug molecular graph is progressively aggregated from atom-level structures to local structural units and higher-level molecular fragments. The coefficient $\beta$ controls the contribution of the MMD loss to the total objective function, thereby regulating the distribution constraint between local hierarchical features and global graph features.

This section analyzes the influence of $n_1/n_2$ and $\beta$ on model performance. It should be noted that $n_1$ and $n_2$ are not purely empirical parameters. Instead, they have explicit structural meanings in the hierarchical graph coarsening process. They correspond to the numbers of retained nodes in the two DiffPool layers applied to the drug molecular graph, and can therefore be interpreted from the perspectives of small-molecule structural composition and graph pooling compression ratio.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity Analysis of $n_1$ and $n_2$}

The hierarchical GNN module of HAG-DTA contains two DiffPool layers and outputs two coarsened graphs. The first coarsened graph is used to learn local structural information from the atom-level molecular graph, while the second coarsened graph further captures higher-level molecular fragment information. In this study, the number of nodes in the first coarsened graph is defined as $n_1$, and the number of nodes in the second coarsened graph is defined as $n_2$. Since this module operates on the drug molecular graph, $n_1$ and $n_2$ essentially control the coarsening degree of ligand-side structural representation, determining how the model progressively forms local structural units and higher-level fragment representations from atom-level structures.

For general small molecules, the molecular graph usually consists of several chemically meaningful local structures, such as aromatic rings, heterocycles, linkers, hydrogen bond donor or acceptor groups, hydrophobic fragments, charged groups, and halogen-substituted regions. These structural units may affect the interaction between the drug molecule and the target binding pocket through hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic interactions, electrostatic interactions, $\pi$-$\pi$ stacking, halogen bonding, or spatial conformational fitting. Therefore, in molecular graph representation, preserving atom-level details helps enhance local expressiveness, but may also introduce redundant information. Excessive graph compression may lead to the loss of key functional groups and local interaction units. The settings of $n_1$ and $n_2$ should thus balance structural preservation and hierarchical abstraction.

From the perspective of graph pooling ratio, $n_1$ should retain a relatively sufficient number of local structural nodes to avoid premature merging of atom clusters with different chemical roles in the first pooling layer. If $n_1$ is too small, multiple functional groups, ring structures, or substituents may be compressed into the same supernode, weakening local structural differences. If $n_1$ is too large, the first coarsened graph remains close to the original atom-level graph, which weakens the hierarchical abstraction effect and may introduce redundant supernodes, increasing the learning difficulty of the model.

By comparison, $n_2$ corresponds to the coarser fragment-level representation after the second pooling layer, which further compresses the local structural units obtained from the first layer. If $n_2$ is too small, the model may fail to retain the synergistic relationships among different molecular fragments. If $n_2$ is too large, the second pooling layer may have limited ability to summarize higher-level molecular regions. Therefore, a reasonable setting is to make $n_2$ smaller than $n_1$ while maintaining an appropriate compression ratio. According to the experimental observations in this study, when $n_2$ is approximately one-half to one-quarter of $n_1$, the model usually achieves relatively stable performance.

These results indicate that the preferred pooling granularity varies across datasets, which may be related to differences in molecular structural complexity, sample size, and task type.

Further observation of different parameter combinations shows that although changes in $n_1$ and $n_2$ lead to certain performance fluctuations, the model maintains good predictive performance within a reasonable parameter range. In particular, when $n_1 \geq 2$, $n_2 < 10$, and $n_2$ is approximately one-half to one-quarter of $n_1$, HAG-DTA usually obtains stable performance. This phenomenon suggests that the hierarchical graph pooling module is more suitable for progressive structural compression. An excessively small coarsened graph may cause structural information loss, while an excessively large coarsened graph may weaken the abstraction ability of the pooling process for higher-level molecular structures.

\subsection{Model interpretation}
During the period from 2015 to 2020, analysis of data concerning 164 small molecule drugs approved by the FDA\cite{bhutani2021us} revealed that 87\% (142 drug molecules) possessed N-heterocycles and aromatic skeletons. Chiral molecules accounted for 63\%. In contrast, the occurrence of heterocyclic oxygen compounds was relatively low. These findings suggest a regularity in the composition of drug molecules, where specific structures confer distinct chemical properties to such molecules. Hence, in the realm of computer-aided drug discovery, comprehending how models extract feature information and understanding the impact of these features on the model's performance can offer valuable insights for optimizing the structure of drug molecules.

The HAG-DTA model utilizes hierarchical GNN networks and deep GNN networks to capture the multi-scale features of drug molecules. By analyzing the cluster allocation matrix and attention scores generated by the model, we can easily understand which functional groups and important atoms are extracted from the drug molecules. We chose the best model trained on the Davis dataset for model interpretation, with hyperparameters $n_1=4$ and $n_2=2$. In this configuration, the first coarsened graph has 4 nodes, while the second coarsened graph has 2 nodes. The model interpretation results for three drug molecules (a), (b), and (c) with CIDs of 9818231, 24779724, and 123631 respectively, in the PubChem database\cite{kim2016pubchem}, are illustrated in Figure 5. Based on the two cluster allocation matrices output by the hierarchical GNN network, we visualized the clustering results. The clustering results of the first layer are labeled with four colors, and the clustering results of the second layer are labeled with black dashed irregular circles, as shown on the left side of \autoref{fig4}. Furthermore, the attention scores for each atom, derived from the output of the deep GNN network, are depicted on the right side of \autoref{fig4}. From the figure, it can be seen that the model extracts the features of the pyrimidine (orange part) and piperidinepropanenitrile (green part) structures of drug molecule (a) during the first layer clustering. During the second layer clustering, the model merges the blue and orange parts and extracts the features of the molecular fragment pyrrole and pyrimidine. Additionally, the model identifies and extracts the functional group information of triazole and quinoline in drug molecule (b), as well as the functional group information of morpholinopropoxy and quinazoline in drug molecule (c). Moreover, the model's global attention scores demonstrate a higher focus on the atoms at the connection points of the two molecular fragments, such as the sulfur atom in drug molecule (b). The above results demonstrate that the HAG-DTA model can accurately identify and extract the features of functional groups and molecular fragments, and it has good interpretability for the DTA task.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
This work presents the HAG-DTA model, which aims to predict Drug-Target Affinity (DTA) by capturing multi-scale feature information of drug molecules. The HAG-DTA model comprises two modules: the hierarchical GNN module and the deep GNN module. The hierarchical GNN module extracts functional groups and molecular fragment features of drug molecules through hierarchical clustering. The deep GNN module employs a gate skipping connection mechanism to fuse multi-level information of molecules and extract global features of drug molecules. Meanwhile, we introduced the MMD loss function to limit the distribution difference between the substructure features and global features. Moreover, the HAG-DTA model incorporates an attention mechanism to adaptively integrate the extracted multi-scale features. Extensive experiments on four publicly available datasets have showcased the superiority of this approach. Furthermore, we performed model interpretation based on hierarchical clustering and attention scores. The results show that the HAG-DTA model exhibits good interpretability, thereby contributing to the advancement of research on drug structure optimization.
